TOPIC: Introduction to Derivatives (Calculus)

Section 1: What is a Derivative?
The derivative of a function measures how the function's output changes as its input
changes. Formally, the derivative of f(x) at a point x is defined as the limit of the
average rate of change over a shrinking interval:

    f'(x) = lim (h -> 0) [f(x+h) - f(x)] / h

This is also called the instantaneous rate of change, and geometrically it represents
the slope of the tangent line to the curve y = f(x) at the point x.

Section 2: The Power Rule
For any function of the form f(x) = x^n, where n is a real number, the derivative is:

    f'(x) = n * x^(n-1)

Example: if f(x) = x^3, then f'(x) = 3x^2.
Example: if f(x) = x, then f'(x) = 1, since x = x^1 and 1 * x^0 = 1.
Example: if f(x) = 5 (a constant), then f'(x) = 0, because a constant function has
a slope of zero everywhere - it never changes.

Section 3: The Sum Rule
The derivative of a sum of functions is the sum of their derivatives:

    d/dx [f(x) + g(x)] = f'(x) + g'(x)

This means you can differentiate each term of a polynomial separately and add the
results together.

Section 4: The Product Rule
When differentiating the product of two functions f(x) and g(x), you cannot simply
multiply the derivatives. Instead:

    d/dx [f(x) * g(x)] = f'(x) * g(x) + f(x) * g'(x)

A common mistake is assuming the derivative of a product is just the product of the
derivatives - this is incorrect and is a frequent source of student errors.

Section 5: The Chain Rule
For composite functions, where one function is nested inside another, the chain rule
states:

    d/dx [f(g(x))] = f'(g(x)) * g'(x)

In words: take the derivative of the outer function (leaving the inner function
unchanged), then multiply by the derivative of the inner function.

Example: if h(x) = (3x + 1)^2, treat (3x+1) as the inner function g(x) and squaring
as the outer function f. Then f'(g(x)) = 2(3x+1) and g'(x) = 3, so
h'(x) = 2(3x+1) * 3 = 6(3x+1) = 18x + 6.

Section 6: Derivatives and Slopes of Tangent Lines
Because f'(x) gives the slope of the tangent line at any point x, we can find the
equation of the tangent line at a specific point x = a using:

    y - f(a) = f'(a) * (x - a)

This connects the algebraic process of differentiation to the geometric picture of
a line just touching the curve at one point.
